That first project was a direct, open-data adaptation of Jura Liaukonyte's work at Cornell, together with Tuchman & Zhu (2023): cross Colombia's 2026 presidential vote with where people shop, eat, and search online, town by town, and ask whether consumption has a political color. It traveled further than I expected, thank you for that.

But that question only looks at consumption from one angle: as a mirror of the vote. It never asks what the market itself looks like, whether it's simple or sophisticated, complete or full of gaps, growing evenly or avoiding certain territory, held together or fractured. So I built a companion project that asks exactly that, and only afterward checks where politics shows up in the answer.

None of the methods below were built for retail, for Colombia, or even for economics in some cases. I borrowed each one from wherever it was already solving a similar problem. No results here, just what each page measures and where the idea comes from:

🔹 Complexity: how sophisticated each municipality's retail basket is, inspired by economic complexity (Hidalgo & Hausmann, 2009).

🔹 Brands: which brands are common and which are rare, inspired by the "product space" (Hidalgo, Klinger, Barabási & Hausmann, 2007).

🔹 Opportunity: which brand a town is "missing," inspired by relatedness density (Hidalgo et al., 2007), plus whether political distance slows a brand's expansion (McConnell et al., 2017; Panagopoulos et al., 2020).

🔹 Access: retail deserts, inspired by a formula built to find medical deserts (2SFCA, Luo & Wang, 2003).

🔹 Integration: how separated shopping is relative to the vote, inspired by segregation indices from neighborhood and school studies (Massey & Denton, 1988; Davis, Dingel, Monras & Morales, 2019).

🔹 Risk: what happens if a brand is seen taking a side, inspired by research on brand activism and partisan boycotts (Hydock, Paharia & Blair, 2020; Hou & Poliquin, 2022).

🔹 Clusters: towns where politics and market structure disagree, inspired by LISA, a spatial statistic built for disease and crime hotspots (Anselin, 1995).

🔹 Network: which brands "live" together, inspired by community-detection math used to find friend groups in social networks (Clauset, Newman & Moore, 2004).

🔹 Uncertainty: how much to trust each town's political data, inspired by a Bayesian model built to smooth small-area disease statistics (Besag, York & Mollié, 1991).

I'm 𝘯𝘰𝘵 going to tell you what any of it found. That's the whole point of building it as an interactive project instead of a table: pick a chain you know, a town you're from, a category you buy from, and read the map yourself.
